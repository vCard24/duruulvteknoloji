Fire-Rated Steel Doors

EI fire resistance ratings, testing and where fire doors are required.

Fire is one of the greatest risks buildings can face. In Turkey, thousands of fires occur every year, and many fatalities result from inadequate safety measures. This is where fire-resistant steel doors matter. How do they differ from standard doors? Where are they mandatory? Let's look at the details.

What is a fire-resistant door?

Fire-resistant doors prevent the spread of fire and smoke for a specified period. They are made from materials that withstand high temperatures and are tested and certified accordingly.

The key difference from a standard steel door is that a fire door is tested and certified under defined standards that measure resistance duration, smoke control, and structural integrity.

Fire door standards and classification

In Turkey, fire doors are tested under TS EN 1634-1 and classified by resistance duration:

Key features

1. Special materials

  • Steel structure: galvanized steel at least 1.5 mm thick
  • Fire insulation materials: mineral wool, vermiculite, or special boards
  • Sealing system: intumescent seals that expand in heat
  • Resistant coating: withstands high temperatures

2. Automatic closing system

  • Mandatory hydraulic or spring closer
  • Must always self-close
  • Hold-open magnets can be linked to fire alarm
  • Closing speed adjustable

3. Smoke sealing

Around 70% of fire fatalities are caused by smoke. Fire doors use special seals:

  • Cold seals: tight fit under normal conditions
  • Intumescent seals: expand to block smoke
  • Threshold seal: prevents smoke from below

4. Panic hardware

  • Panic bar
  • Single-motion exit from inside
  • Locked from outside, free from inside
  • Integration with emergency lighting

Where are they mandatory?

Mandatory use areas

Building type Requirement Minimum rating
Buildings 5+ floors Stairwell entrance EI 30
Shopping centers Fire compartment doors EI 60
Hospitals All compartment doors EI 60–90
Schools Stair and corridor doors EI 30–60
Hotels Fire separation doors EI 60
Factories Production area entrances EI 60–120
Enclosed parking Building entrance EI 60

Recommended areas

  • Villas and luxury residences
  • Basement floors
  • Boiler rooms
  • Electrical panel rooms
  • Archives and storage areas

Testing and certification

Test stages

  1. Fire resistance test: exposure to high heat for the rated duration
  2. Smoke leakage test
  3. Heat transfer test
  4. Structural integrity test
  5. Automatic closing test

Required documentation

  • TS EN 1634-1 test report
  • CE certificate
  • Fire performance certificate
  • Factory quality certificate
  • Installation and operation manual

What to consider when choosing

1. Correct resistance duration

Choose the EI class suited to building use and regulations. Longer is not always better if it adds cost without need.

2. Verify certifications

Request test reports and certificates. Fake certifications create legal problems.

3. Installation quality

Fire door performance depends on correct installation by a certified team.

4. Maintenance

Professional inspection at least twice a year is recommended.

Pricing and cost analysis

Fire door prices may be 30–100% higher than standard steel doors:

  • Size: larger doors cost more

A fire door is an investment in life safety and legal compliance, and may reduce insurance premiums.

Installation

Before installation

  • Check opening dimensions
  • Inspect wall thickness and quality
  • Plan electrical connections
  • Fire alarm integration

During installation

  • Precise frame fitting
  • Full seal placement
  • Closer adjustment
  • Gap filling with fire-rated materials

After installation

  • Opening and closing tests
  • Seal inspection
  • Closer adjustment
  • Installation documentation

Periodic maintenance

Monthly check (user)

  • Full closure
  • Closer operation
  • Lock condition
  • Visible damage

Semi-annual maintenance

  • Inspect and lubricate hinges
  • Adjust closer
  • Check seals
  • Lock maintenance

Annual inspection

  • Inspect all mechanisms
  • Visual fire-resistance check
  • Update certificates
  • Maintenance report

Legal responsibilities

Building owner duties

  • Use fire doors that meet regulations
  • Periodic maintenance and inspection
  • Keep certificates on file
  • Maintain a fire safety plan

Penalties

  • Administrative fines
  • Facility closure
  • Criminal liability in case of fire
  • Insurance claim rejection

Frequently asked questions

Can a standard steel door be used as a fire door?

No. The door must be tested and certified.

How long does resistance last?

30, 60, 90, or 120 minutes by class, under approved test conditions.

Can it be kept open?

No. If it must stay open, use a magnet linked to the fire alarm.

How long is the certificate valid?

The certificate has no expiry date, but periodic maintenance is essential.

Is it mandatory in older buildings?

During renovation or change of use, fire safety measures must be updated.

Conclusion

Fire-resistant steel doors are not merely a legal obligation; they are a critical measure to protect lives. With the right choice, proper installation, and regular maintenance, they play a life-saving role.

The right time to act is before a fire occurs.
